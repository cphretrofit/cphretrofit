Building Regulations Part F Section 2 of 6

What is Part F?

Part F of the Building Regulations (England) covers Ventilation. It sets standards for:

  • Removing pollutants and moisture from inside buildings
  • Providing fresh air for occupants
  • Preventing condensation and mould growth
  • Ensuring systems operate efficiently
‚ö†Ô∏è Legal Requirement: Compliance with Part F is mandatory. Non-compliance can result in enforcement action, invalidation of warranties, and serious liability issues if occupant health is affected.

The Three Ventilation Strategies

1. Natural Ventilation

How: Trickle vents, windows, air bricks

Best for: Traditional construction, low airtightness

Retrofit note: Often insufficient for highly insulated homes. May need upgrading.

2. Mechanical Extract Ventilation (MEV)

How: Central fan continuously extracts from wet rooms

Best for: Moderate airtightness (3-5 m¬≥/hr¬∑m¬≤ @ 50Pa)

Retrofit note: Common upgrade path. Low energy, effective moisture control.

3. Mechanical Ventilation with Heat Recovery (MVHR)

How: Balanced supply and extract with heat recovery

Best for: High airtightness (<3 m¬≥/hr¬∑m¬≤ @ 50Pa), Passivhaus standard

Ventilation Systems Explained Section 3 of 6

Mechanical Extract Ventilation (MEV)

Centralised system with ducting to multiple extract points.

Advantages:
  • Lower cost than MVHR (¬£1,500-3,000 vs ¬£4,000-8,000)
  • Simpler installation (no supply ducting)
  • Effective moisture removal
  • Lower maintenance requirements
Limitations:
  • No heat recovery‚Äîextracted heat is lost
  • Can create negative pressure (draws in cold air through gaps)
  • Requires background ventilators (trickle vents) in habitable rooms
  • Less effective in very airtight homes

Mechanical Ventilation with Heat Recovery (MVHR)

Balanced system extracting stale air and supplying fresh air via heat exchanger.

üîÑ

How MVHR Works

1. Warm, stale air extracted from kitchen, bathrooms, utility
2. Passes through heat exchanger (ceramic or plate)
3. Heat transfers to incoming fresh air without mixing
4. Fresh, warmed air supplied to living rooms, bedrooms
5. Heat recovery efficiency: 80-95%

Advantages:
  • Excellent energy efficiency‚Äîrecovers most heat
  • Constant fresh air supply to all rooms
  • Filters pollen and dust (benefits allergy sufferers)
  • No need for trickle vents (cleaner aesthetic)
  • Best air quality control
Critical Requirements:
  • Home must be airtight (<3 m¬≥/hr¬∑m¬≤ @ 50Pa) or efficiency is lost
  • Requires space for ducting and unit (often loft or cupboard)
  • Must be commissioned and balanced correctly
  • Filters need regular changing (every 6-12 months)
  • Condensate drainage required

Decentralised MVHR (dMVHR)

Single-room units (e.g., Vent-Axia Lo-Carbon Tempra) that recover heat without ducting.

Use case: Retrofit where ducting is difficult. Each room has its own unit. Less efficient than centralised but better than extract-only.

Intermittent Extract Fans

Traditional bathroom/kitchen fans operating on switches, timers, or humidity sensors.

Part F Compliance Note: Intermittent fans can comply with Part F, but only if they meet specific flow rates and are used with background ventilators. In retrofitted homes, continuous mechanical ventilation is usually preferable.

CPH Retrofit Installation Standards

Ducting Rules: Rigid ducting preferred over flexible. If flexible used, must be fully extended (no sagging). Maximum 90¬∞ bends‚Äîuse swept bends where possible. Insulate ducting in cold lofts to prevent condensation.

Commissioning Requirements

Every mechanical ventilation system must be commissioned (balanced and tested):

  1. Measure actual flow rates at each grille/valve
  2. Adjust dampers to achieve design flows
  3. Check heat recovery efficiency (MVHR)
  4. Measure specific fan power (SFP must be <0.6 W/l/s for MVHR)
  5. Issue commissioning certificate (mandatory for Building Control)
‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: An uncommissioned system is non-compliant. CPH Retrofit requires commissioning on every project. The certificate must be filed with project documentation.

Common Installation Errors

‚ùå Wrong

  • Ducting crushed or kinked
  • Excessive bends (>3 per run)
  • Uninsulated ducts in cold spaces
  • Intake near extractor outlet
  • No condensate trap on MVHR
  • Filters not fitted

‚úì Right

  • Smooth, rigid ducting throughout
  • Gentle bends, maximum 3 per run
  • 25mm insulation minimum on cold side
  • Intake & exhaust >1m apart
  • Condensate drain with trap
  • Filters fitted and dated

Handover to Occupants

Every ventilation system installation must include user training:

  • How to boost ventilation (cooking, showering)

Troubleshooting Common Issues

üîá System Noisy

Causes: Fan imbalance, duct vibration, blocked filter

Action: Check filters, ensure ducts secured with anti-vibration mounts, check fan mounting

üíß Condensation on Windows

Causes: System not running, filters blocked, boost not used

Action: Check power, change filters, educate occupants on boost use

ü•∂ Cold Draughts

Causes: Supply vents too close to seating, flow rates too high, no heat recovery

Action: Adjust dampers, relocate vents if possible, check commissioning

‚ö° High Energy Bills

Causes: Filters blocked (fan working harder), system oversized, SFP too high

Action: Replace filters, check fan power settings, consider upgrade if old system

‚ö†Ô∏è Mould Warning: If occupants report mould after retrofit, ventilation is the first suspect. Check: Is system running? Are filters clean? Is boost being used? Is the home more airtight than the ventilation can handle?
